Interpretation

Across all analyses, a consistent pattern emerged linking personality traits with patterns of drug use.

1. Core Personality Traits Predict Substance Use

Across nearly all drug categories, four traits showed the strongest and most consistent associations:

  • Openness ‚Äì the strongest positive predictor of both trying and frequently using substances. Users scored significantly higher across cannabis, nicotine, amphetamines, benzodiazepines, stimulants, and psychedelics.
  • Neuroticism ‚Äì elevated among users of most drug categories, especially stimulants, benzodiazepines, and high-risk drugs. Higher usage frequency was also positively correlated with neuroticism.
  • Agreeableness ‚Äì consistently lower among users, indicating less compliance, cooperativeness, and prosocial behavior.
  • Conscientiousness ‚Äì strongly negatively associated with all forms of drug use, reflecting reduced impulse control and lower behavioral regulation.

Extraversion showed the weakest and least consistent effects, aligning with findings from previous literature.

2. Legal vs. Illegal Substances Show Distinct Usage Patterns

Chi-squared tests and exploratory visualizations revealed sharp differences between legal and illegal substances:

  • Legal drugs (alcohol, nicotine) had higher consumption levels overall and higher frequencies in the medium-to-heavy range.
  • Illegal drugs (cannabis, stimulants, psychedelics, high-risk opioids) showed high proportions of users in the low-frequency range, suggesting sporadic experimentation.

This pattern likely reflects availability, cultural norms, perceived risk, and legal access.

3. Stimulants (Cocaine + Meth) Show a Distinctive Personality Profile

The combined stimulant group demonstrated some of the strongest personality effects:

  • Higher Neuroticism
  • Lower Agreeableness and Conscientiousness
  • Moderate increases in Openness

These results align with established psychological risk profiles for stimulant misuse, particularly impulsivity and emotional dysregulation.

4. Psychedelic Use Is Most Strongly Linked to Openness

Psychedelic users (LSD, mushrooms, ketamine, ecstasy, legal highs) showed:

  • Large positive effects on Openness
  • Moderately lower Agreeableness and Conscientiousness
  • Minimal involvement of Extraversion

This suggests that curiosity, novelty-seeking, and cognitive exploration are major drivers of psychedelic experimentation.

5. High-Risk Drug Use (Heroin, Crack, VSA)

Although relatively rare in the dataset, users of high-risk drugs exhibited:

  • Strongly elevated Neuroticism
  • Markedly lower Agreeableness and Conscientiousness

This reflects known clinical patterns: reduced impulse control, emotional instability, and high behavioral risk-taking.

6. Overall Synthesis

Across all drug categories and statistical methods, three overarching themes emerged:

  • Openness predicts trying more types of substances.
  • Neuroticism predicts more frequent or heavier use.
  • Agreeableness and Conscientiousness protect against use across nearly all substances.

Combined with the influence of education and country-level factors, these results suggest that personality traits and sociocultural context jointly shape patterns of drug experimentation and use. While causation cannot be inferred, the consistency of patterns across multiple substances underscores the stability of these psychological profiles.
