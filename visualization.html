Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

Our exploratory data analysis (EDA) begins by transforming the cleaned Fehrman et al. dataset into several analysis-ready versions. We collapse the original seven-level CL0‚ÄìCL6 drug-use scale into binary indicators of user vs. non-user, define a harmful user indicator that excludes caffeine and chocolate, and classify users as legal-only vs. illegal based on whether they report using drugs beyond alcohol, caffeine, chocolate, and nicotine.

Using these derived variables, we:

  • Tabulate the characteristics of the study sample.
  • Visualize the distributions of the Big Five (OCEAN) personality scores, impulsivity, and sensation seeking.
  • Compare personality scores between non-users and users (and harmful users) using boxplots.
  • Examine how the proportion of legal vs. illegal users changes across levels of impulsivity and sensation seeking.
  • Compute a correlation matrix between personality traits and ordinal drug-usage levels and display it as a heatmap.

Overall, the EDA suggests that while Big Five scores for users and non-users overlap substantially, higher sensation seeking, impulsivity, and openness are moderately associated with greater usage of several mostly illegal drugs (such as cannabis, legal highs, ecstasy, LSD, and mushrooms), whereas higher conscientiousness tends to be weakly associated with lower cannabis and legal-high usage.

Summary of Visualization & Modeling Results

We first created a total drug consumption score for each participant by summing ordinal use levels across 18 drugs, and defined a binary ‚Äúuser / non-user‚Äù indicator for every substance. A chi-squared test comparing legal vs illegal drugs showed a strong association between drug type and consumption level: legal drugs (alcohol, nicotine, caffeine, etc.) are much more likely to be used at higher frequencies, while illegal drugs are concentrated at ‚Äúnever / very rare‚Äù levels.

A linear regression using only the set of drugs a person uses predicts their log-transformed total consumption very well (adjusted R¬≤ ‚âà 0.88). When we relate total use to personality traits, higher openness, neuroticism, impulsivity, and sensation seeking‚Äîand lower conscientiousness and agreeableness‚Äîare all associated with heavier drug use, whereas extraversion shows almost no association. Personality alone, however, explains only a modest fraction of the variability in total consumption.

We then added environmental and demographic factors. Total consumption differs significantly by education, country, gender, ethnicity, and age. Higher risk is seen among people aged 18‚Äì24, those with ‚Äúsome college or university, no certificate or degree,‚Äù residents of the U.S., men, and several mixed/‚Äúother‚Äù ethnic groups. In contrast, participants from the U.K. and older age groups generally show lower consumption and more protective personality profiles.

Our final ‚Äúbest‚Äù linear model combines key personality traits (O, C, N, and sensation seeking), education, age, gender, country, and selected interactions (including country‚Äìethnicity). Overall, our visualizations and models suggest that personality is important, but social and demographic context also plays a major role in shaping drug use patterns.
